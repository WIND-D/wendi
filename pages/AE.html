Redesigning Data Visulization for Antibiotics Experiment in Year 1951

With each antibiotic, on which bacteria it is most effective being used?

COURSE PROJECT

Project Type:  Data Visulization

Roles:  Designer & Programmer

Team:  Individual work

Timeline:  Spring 2018

After World War II, antibiotics earned the moniker “wonder drugs” for quickly treating previously-incurable diseases. Data was gathered to determine which drug worked best for each bacterial infection. Comparing drug performance was an enormous aid for practitioners and scientists alike. In the fall of 1951, Will Burtin published this graph showing the effectiveness of three popular antibiotics on 16 different bacteria, measured in terms of minimum inhibitory concentration.

Fig.1 Antibiotics Experiment (1951) Data

The experiment’s results answered below questions:
Q1: Which antibiotic is the most effective one on every bacteria?
Q2: For gram-positive, gram-negative bacteria (Bacterial spices are classified into two groups according to Gram Staining technique), which antibiotic prevents growth of the bacteria group more effectively?

By comparing different MIC value in a row or a column of the raw data (Fig.1), we can easily pick out the answer of Q1, which has the least MIC of the compared set. But for Q2, it’s hard to tell the answer by just scanning all the MIC and gram staining result. To better figure out insights of the experiment’s raw data, data visualization “Effectiveness of Antibiotics” at the top of this page is designed.
